# Some history

On the Christmas day (25 december) of 1640, [Pierre de Fermat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_de_Fermat) wrote a letter to [Marin Mersenne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marin_Mersenne) where he stated that every prime number of the form $ 4n+1 $ could be written as the sum of two squares. He claimed to have an irrefutable proof of the statement. 

The result was already known to [Albert Girard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Girard), but the first complete proof was given by [Leonhard Euler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonhard_Euler), who announced it in a letter sent to [Christian Goldbach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christian_Goldbach).

Today the theorem is known as "Fermat's theorem on sums of two squares" or "Fermat's Christmas Theorem" because of the date in which Fermat claimed to have the proof.

Detailed historical information can be found in the relative chapter of {cite}`dickinson_history_1938`.

>$$

>$$
>where $ a $ and $ b $ are integers?

Since we have some idea about the previous two questions but we don't have any idea about the latter one, we start to focus on it.

The first thing we do is to proceed by trials and errors in order to understand better the problem.

Consider the prime number $ 2 $. Can we write it as the sum of two squares? Well, there aren't a lot of ways to write $ 2 $ as sum of something else:
$$
    2=1+1
$$
But since $ 1^2=1 $ we have
$$
    2=1^2+1^2
$$
therefore we can indeed write $ 2 $ as the sum of two squares.

Let's try with $ 3 $: the only possible way of writing $ 3 $ as a sum of two numbers is $ 3=1+2 $. However $ 2 $ is not a square, so $ 3 $ does not satisfy the property we are interested in.

We move on the number $ 5 $: we can write it as $ 5=2+3 $, but since $ 2 $ and $ 3 $ are not squares, we have to search for another writing, that we find in $ 5=4+1 $, since $ 4=2^2 $ and $ 1=1^2 $.
$$
    5=1^2+2^2.
$$

What about $ 7 $? Well, we can write $ 7 $ as:
- $ 7=1+6 $, but $ 6 $ is not a square;
- $ 7=2+5 $, but neither $ 2 $ nor $ 5 $ are squares;
- $ 7=3+4 $, but $ 3 $ is not a square.
therefore $ 7 $ cannot be written as the sum of two squares.
