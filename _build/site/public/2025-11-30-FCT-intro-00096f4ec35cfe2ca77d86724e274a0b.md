# Some history

On the Christmas day (25 december) of 1640, [Pierre de Fermat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_de_Fermat) wrote a letter to [Marin Mersenne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marin_Mersenne) where he stated that every prime number of the form $ 4n+1 $ could be written as the sum of two squares. He claimed to have an irrefutable proof of the statement. 

The result was already known to [Albert Girard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Girard), but the first complete proof was given by [Leonhard Euler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonhard_Euler), who announced it in a letter sent to [Christian Goldbach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christian_Goldbach).

Today the theorem is known as "Fermat's theorem on sums of two squares" or "Fermat's Christmas Theorem" because of the date in which Fermat claimed to have the proof.

Detailed historical information can be found in the relative chapter of {cite}`dickinson_history_1938`.

# Understanding the problem
Before diving in the statement of the theorem, let us understand the problem. All of us know the [Pythagorean theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagorean_theorem), which says that in a right triangle

:::{figure} ./images/right_triangle.svg
:::

we have $ a^2+b^2=c^2 $. In general $ a,b $ and $ c $ could be any number, for instance we can choose $ a=3 $, $ b=4 $, and $ c=5 $ and we would have 
$$
    3^2+4^2=9+16=25=5^2
$$
or we could also choose $ a=1 $, $ b=1 $ and $ c=\sqrt 2 $, so that
$$
    1^2+1^2=2=(\sqrt 2)^2
$$

In this context we ask $ a,b $ and $ c $ to be integer numbers, which are 
$$
\dots, -3,-2,-1,0,1,2,3,\dots 
$$
In particular, we don't consider numbers like $ 1/3 $, $ 1.78 $, $ \sqrt 2 $, $ \sqrt 3\dots $ 

Thus, examples such as $ a=1, $ $ b=1 $, and $ c=\sqrt 2 $ will not be the focus of our study.


In this context we could ask: what are all the possible combinations $ a,b,c $ of integers that are edges of a right triangle? 

We will answer this question in a precise way, and we will characterize all of such integers.

:::{prf:remark}
Observe that we are allowing $ a,b,c $ to be negative integers, therefore what are we studying is apparently more general than what happens in the geometric context (where the length are of course positive). Consider for instance the three integers $ a=-3 $, $ b=4 $, and $ c=-5 $. We get
$$
    (-3)^2+4^2=(-5)^2
$$
similarly to the first example.

Indeed, since we are working with squares, the sign of our number isn't really important to us (because $ (-3)^2=3^2 $ and $ (-5)^2=5^2 $) thus we can always think about the geometric picture, where everything is positive, without losing any information.
:::



**What can we say when we ask $ c $ to be a prime number?**




Let's dive into the statement of the theorem and try to understand it. 

:::{prf:theorem} Fermat's Christmas Theorem
A prime number $ p $ can be written as the sum of two squares if and only if it is of the form $ p=4n+1 $ .
:::
